## U.S. national news

1. A federal judge on June 25, 2026 blocked large parts of President Donald Trump's March election executive order. AP reported the ruling said the president cannot force documentary proof of citizenship onto the federal voter-registration form and cannot require all mailed ballots to arrive by Election Day. The decision matters because it limits unilateral federal changes to election administration and leaves state-run election rules in place while appeals are likely.
2. The U.S. Supreme Court issued two 6-3 immigration rulings on June 25, 2026 that favored the administration. One lets the government move ahead with ending Temporary Protected Status for people from Haiti and Syria, potentially affecting more than 350,000 people. Another allows the administration to revive "metering" at ports of entry, limiting how many asylum seekers can be processed each day. These decisions matter immediately because they reshape who can stay lawfully in the United States and how quickly asylum claims can be presented at the border.
3. Senate Republicans defeated a war-powers resolution on June 25, 2026 by a 47-50-1 vote after internal party pressure around President Trump's Iran policy. AP described it as a test of whether Congress would try to limit the president's authority in the conflict. The near-term watch item is whether any further military action or congressional oversight fight changes that balance.

## Virginia Beach

1. Virginia Beach City Council adopted the revised FY 2027 budget on June 25, 2026. The city says the combined city-and-schools budget totals about $2.886 billion, includes up to $80 in personal-property-tax relief per vehicle, and provides an approximately 3.5% salary increase for city employees beginning July 1. This matters because it sets the local tax, pay, and service baseline for the coming fiscal year.
2. The city also published a June 25, 2026 explainer on fees and rates taking effect July 1. Verified examples include a water service availability fee of $6.30 per month, a water usage rate of $6.54 per 1,000 gallons, no sanitary-sewer increase, a trash-can replacement fee of $100, and a resident recreation-center annual membership of $125. This matters today because households may see the changes on bills and program costs almost immediately.
3. WTKR reported on June 25, 2026 that no charges were filed after the June 22 Strawbridge Marketplace barricade situation. Police said the person is seeking active mental-health treatment, tear gas was deployed, the person was taken into custody Monday, and nobody was hurt. The story matters locally because it closed out a visible public-safety incident without injuries while highlighting the mental-health component.

## Kyoto

1. The Japan Meteorological Agency reported that an earthquake occurred at 10:44 p.m. Japan time on June 25, 2026, centered in southern Kyoto Prefecture. Magnitude was 3.1, maximum intensity was shindo 2 in Kameoka, several Kyoto City wards observed shindo 1, depth was about 20 km, and there was no tsunami risk. This matters mainly as a public-safety reminder rather than a damage event.
2. Kyoto City announced stronger anti-litter measures for the Gion area, Kiyamachi-dori, and related districts during the Gion Festival period. The June 25, 2026 release signals extra waste countermeasures in one of the city's most crowded festival windows. It matters because the city is trying to protect daily life and the streetscape while tourism pressure rises.
3. Kyoto City released two culture-and-transit items on June 25, 2026: Kyoto International Manga Anime Fair 2026 will run a crowdfunding campaign tied to its 15th anniversary and partial exhibitor information is now public, and the municipal subway will hold a first-time driving experience event for the new Karasuma Line Series 20 trains. These are not emergency items, but they are meaningful local developments in how Kyoto is presenting culture and public transport to residents and visitors.

## Mature women's health

1. The Menopause Society highlighted a June 18, 2026 observational study showing that menopausal women using hormone therapy also reported healthier eating, physical activity, and sleep patterns. The important caution is that this was an association study, not proof that hormone therapy caused those healthier behaviors.
2. NCCIH states that convincing research supporting detox diets or cleanses for toxin removal or weight management is limited, and some programs may be unsafe or falsely advertised. Verified risks include dehydration, electrolyte imbalance, inadequate nutrition, and medication interactions. The body normally relies on the liver, kidneys, lungs, skin, and gastrointestinal tract for waste processing.
3. The U.S. Office on Women's Health and ACOG continue to frame menopause treatment as individualized. Established options include lifestyle measures, hormone therapy for appropriate patients, and nonhormonal medicines; persistent, severe, sudden, or concerning symptoms merit clinician review rather than self-directed cleanse products.
